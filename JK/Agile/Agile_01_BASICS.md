# Agile_01_BASICS

## The Alphabet of Agile

*Target Audience*: Absolute beginner (Grade school student / Non-technical person).  
*Philosophy*: Assume NO prior knowledge. Explain "what" and "why" before "how".

### Installation & Setup

**What is Agile?**  
Agile is like a flexible game plan for getting things done, especially in teams. Instead of following a strict, unchanging schedule, Agile lets you adapt and change as you go, just like adjusting your path while hiking based on the weather or obstacles.

**Why do we need Agile?**  
Traditional ways of doing projects can be like building a house without checking if the foundation is solid â€“ you might end up with problems later. Agile helps teams work better together, deliver results faster, and fix issues quickly. It's used in software development, but also in other areas like planning events or solving problems.

**How to get started with Agile?**  
You don't need special software to start. Begin by learning the ideas through books like "Agile for Dummies" or free online videos. For practicing, use simple tools like sticky notes and a whiteboard to plan small tasks.

**Fundamental Syntax & Keywords**  
Agile has key ideas:  
- **Sprint**: A short period (like 2 weeks) to complete work.  
- **Backlog**: A list of things to do.  
- **Scrum**: A popular Agile way with roles like Scrum Master and Product Owner.  
- **Kanban**: A method using boards to track work flow.  
- **Retrospective**: A meeting to learn from what happened.

**Basic Data Types**  
Agile uses simple information:  
- **Tasks/Items**: Things to do (like "draw a picture").  
- **Time**: Days or weeks for sprints.  
- **People**: Roles like team members or leaders.

**Simple Logic**  
Basic rules:  
- **If/Else**: If a task is done, move to next; else, keep working.  
- **Loops**: Repeat sprints to improve.

**Analogy-heavy explanations**  
Think of Agile as cooking a meal with friends. You plan ingredients (backlog), cook in short bursts (sprints), taste and adjust (feedback), and discuss what worked (retrospective). If the recipe needs changing, you adapt, just like Agile teams do. Another analogy: Agile is like a road trip. You have a destination (goal), but you check the map, adjust for traffic, and enjoy the journey, rather than sticking rigidly to a plan that might lead to a dead end.
